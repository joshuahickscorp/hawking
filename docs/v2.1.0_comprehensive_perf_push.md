# v2.1.0 — comprehensive perf push to 30+ dec_tps on V2-Lite

**Status:** strategy doc, not yet dispatched. This is the plan that
designs how we actually close the gap to llama.cpp-class throughput
on V2-Lite Q4_K_M. It assumes v2.0 has shipped and we have ≥1 week of
real-user feedback in hand.

**Author:** Opus (manager), 2026-05-13
**Supersedes:** `docs/v2.1_architecture_sketch.md` (kept as input; this
plan reframes ANE as one lever among several and corrects the prior
plan's misreading of the TCB trace).

---

## TL;DR

- Realistic v2.1 close: **30–40 dec_tps on V2-Lite** (from 17.3).
- The biggest single lever is **MoE Q4_K GEMV bandwidth utilization**
  (likely 1.5–2× alone), which prior phases ignored because the
  dispatch trace was misread.
- ANE remains valuable (~1.3×) but it's Tier 3, not Tier 1.
- 60 dec_tps is plausible if Tier 1+2+3 all land cleanly. Not promised.

## 1 — Where we actually stand (the honest math)

### 1.1 Current measurement

Latest authoritative bench (commit `c5cff1b`, 2026-05-14):

```
V2-Lite Q4_K_M, M3 Pro 18 GB, coexist TRIALS=4 TOKENS=24
median dec_tps:        17.33
trimmed mean:          17.38
95% CI:                [17.25, 17.42]
IQR:                   0.10
```

Tight CI, well-converged. This is our anchor.

### 1.2 The bandwidth ceiling

DeepSeek-V2-Lite Q4_K_M arithmetic per token:

| component | size | bytes/token (read) |
|---|---|---:|
| Active expert weights (6 of 64 experts × 27 layers × 3 matmuls) | Q4_K (~4.6 bits/wt) | ~1.20 GB |
| Attention projections (q_a, kv_a, q_b, o_proj × 27 layers) | Q4_K | ~95 MB |
| Shared expert weights (always-on) | Q4_K | ~30 MB |
| KV cache reads (MLA, growing with context) | f32, ~1k context avg | ~80 MB |
| LM head | f16 (2048 × 102400) | 419 MB |
| **Total read per token** | | **~1.82 GB** |

M3 Pro 18 GB unified memory: vendor spec 150 GB/s, practical sustained
~120–135 GB/s observed in Apple's own benchmarks.

**Theoretical max**: 130 GB/s ÷ 1.82 GB/token = **~71 dec_tps**.

That's the hard ceiling on this hardware for this model at this quant.
60 dec_tps (llama.cpp) is 84% of that. We're at 17 = 24% utilization.

### 1.3 Where llama.cpp's 3.5× gap comes from

| factor | dismantle | llama.cpp | gap |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| MoE Q4_K GEMV effective bandwidth | est. ~25 GB/s | est. ~85 GB/s | **3.4×** |
| LM head GEMV effective bandwidth | est. ~115 GB/s | est. ~125 GB/s | 1.1× |
| Attention compute (MLA vs flash) | comparable | comparable | ~1× |
| Per-kernel dispatch overhead | 4.1% of decode | <1% | 1.03× |

These bandwidth estimates are derived from per-kernel `bench-kernel`
mean times divided into the kernel's read footprint. They are
**inferences, not direct measurements** — the first job of Tier 1 is
to replace these with real GPU counter data.

But the directional conclusion is solid: **the gap is concentrated in
MoE Q4_K GEMV bandwidth utilization**, with smaller wins available
from LM head, dispatch, and architectural levers.

## 2 — Why prior phases didn't deliver (root cause analysis)

This is the most important section in this doc. If we don't understand
why v1.1.0 Phases 1–5 landed at 17 instead of the projected 30–40, we
will repeat those mistakes.

### 2.1 The TCB trace measurement bug

The prior plans cited this trace as gospel:

```
tcb_commit                   56  3.22s  (69.4%)
gemv_f16 (LM head)           20  1.41s  (30.3%)
moe_batched_gemm_q4_indexed   2,360  4.1ms  (0.1%)
```

And concluded "MoE is 0.1%, don't optimize it; LM head is 30%, attack
that." **This was wrong.**

What the trace actually measures: `moe_batched_gemm_q4_indexed` is
**CPU-side dispatch time** for that kernel (the time spent encoding
the dispatch into the CB). The GPU compute time for MoE is buried
inside the 69.4% `tcb_commit` slice — which is *the entire forward
pass GPU wait*.

The architecture audit (`reports/v1.1.0_architecture_audit.md`)
flagged this explicitly:

> "We cannot currently determine which specific kernel takes the most
> time inside the TCB."

But the conclusion drawn from the trace ("MoE is small, attack the
LM head and dispatch") propagated through Phase 5 (LM head top-K),
Phase 1B (n-gram spec), and the v2.1 sketch (ANE LM head as Tier 1).

**The actual top-line distribution**, inferred from per-kernel
bench-kernel timing × call count, is something like:

| component | est. share of decode time |
|---|---:|
| MoE expert GEMV (gate+up+down × 6 experts × 27 layers) | **55–65%** |
| Attention projections (q_a, kv_a, q_b, o_proj × 27 layers) | **15–20%** |
| LM head GEMV | **5–8%** (post-fold into TCB) |
| MLA decode + softmax | 5–10% |
| RmsNorm + residual + everything else | 3–5% |
| Dispatch overhead | 1–4% |

That's a totally different optimization landscape.

### 2.2 Per-kernel optimizations didn't compound

Phase 1 ported llama.cpp's Q4_K GEMV. Phase 2 added MPSGraph LM head.
Phase 5 added top-K LM head and fp16 KV. Each won 0–5% in isolation;
combined they delivered +0.86% (within noise).

Why: each opt landed as **opt-in only** because it didn't clear the
+5% bench-first gate as the *first* feature. The interaction effects
(does port + MPSGraph compound? does fp16 KV compound with port?)
were never measured. Each feature stayed parked.

This is a methodology issue, not a hard architectural one. Several
opt-ins likely *would* compound if they were composed correctly. We
just never tried.

### 2.3 Spec decode bet on a non-existent foundation

Phase 4 wired n-gram speculative decode and called it a 30–40% e2e
unlock. Sub-phase 1B measured it at α=1.0 on repetitive text:
**−0.5% e2e.** Root cause: `forward_tokens_batched_tcb` is
sequential-in-CB, not K-parallel compute. The "batched verify" saves
only the CB-commit overhead.

Building true K-parallel batched verify is 3–4 weeks of work that was
never scoped. Spec decode was an architectural bet placed without
checking whether the foundation existed.

### 2.4 No GPU-side bandwidth measurement, ever

Every claim of the form "we're at 20% bandwidth utilization" is an
inference from `dec_tps × bytes_per_token / theoretical_max`. We have
never run Instruments' Metal counter template, never read an MTLDevice
counter buffer, never compared our GPU-side time to a calibrated upper
bound.

This means we can't tell whether a kernel optimization is:
(a) actually helping bandwidth utilization,
(b) helping compute throughput but bottlenecked elsewhere, or
(c) shifting cost to another kernel that runs concurrently in TCB.

Without ground-truth GPU timing we are flying blind.

### 2.5 Summary of root causes

1. **Wrong attribution** of where decode time goes (TCB trace
   mislabel) → optimized the wrong things.
2. **No interaction-effect measurement** → opts stayed opt-in,
   never compounded.
3. **Architectural bet without foundation check** → spec decode
   built on sequential verify.
4. **No real bandwidth instrumentation** → can't tell what's working.

All four are fixable. This plan starts by fixing them.

## 3 — The plan, in four tiers

### Tier 1 — measurement infrastructure (1 week, 0% e2e gain)

Foundation. Without these we keep optimizing blind.

#### 3.1.1 GPU-side per-kernel timing

Wire MTLCounterSampleBuffer into TCB so each kernel dispatch records
GPU-side begin/end timestamps to a counter buffer. Read back at
`tcb_commit` time and emit per-kernel **GPU** time alongside the
existing CPU dispatch time.

- New trace mode: `DISMANTLE_TCB_TRACE=gpu` writes
  `bench_results/tcb_gpu_trace_<commit>.jsonl` with one row per
  kernel per token: `{kernel, gpu_begin_ns, gpu_end_ns, cpu_dispatch_ns}`.
- Per-token rollup: kernel × call_count × mean_gpu_us.
- Apple's MTLCounterSampleBuffer is well-documented; this is ~200 LoC
  in `metal/context.rs` and `metal/tcb.rs`.

**Output**: real distribution of GPU time across MoE GEMV, attention
projections, MLA, LM head, etc. for V2-Lite. Replaces the misleading
dispatch trace as the authoritative source for "what's hot."

#### 3.1.2 Bandwidth utilization measurement

For each kernel, compute `effective_bandwidth_GBps = bytes_read /
gpu_time_s`. Compare to M3 Pro's measured peak (run a memory-bound
microbench: pure-read kernel reading 1 GB sequentially, measure GBps).

Per-kernel report: `tools/bench/kernel_bandwidth.sh` produces a
table of every hot kernel with its bandwidth utilization.

#### 3.1.3 llama.cpp comparison harness

Build llama.cpp on the same machine. Run V2-Lite Q4_K_M with `--time`
flag to get llama.cpp's per-token forward time. Use Instruments
gputrace on llama.cpp under the same workload to extract per-kernel
GPU time. Compare to ours kernel-by-kernel.

This is research, not a kernel port. It tells us *which* of llama.cpp's
techniques to copy and *which* don't matter for our workload.

#### 3.1.4 Bench-server bug fix

Sub-phase 1A discovered `dismantle bench-server` overstates dec_tps
vs `dismantle generate` CLI. Root cause: bench-server warmup pollutes
the timing window. Fix or document; pick one and stick with it for
authoritative numbers.

#### Tier 1 deliverables

- `DISMANTLE_TCB_TRACE=gpu` trace mode writing GPU-side per-kernel time
- `tools/bench/kernel_bandwidth.sh` reporting effective bandwidth
- `reports/v2.1.0_kernel_attribution.md` — authoritative breakdown of
  where decode time actually goes
- `reports/v2.1.0_llamacpp_comparison.md` — kernel-by-kernel gap
  analysis
- bench-server bug resolved

**Estimate**: 1 week solo Opus + 1 Haiku worker for the GPU counter
plumbing (mechanical work, parity-tested). Bench-server fix is 1
day. llama.cpp comparison is 2-3 days of measurement + writeup.

**Bench gate**: none. This tier ships infra; perf doesn't change.

**Why this is non-negotiable**: every other tier's success depends on
knowing what to attack. Skipping Tier 1 reproduces the v1.1.0 mistake.

### Tier 2 — close the bandwidth gap on hot kernels (3–5 weeks, target +50–100% e2e)

This is where the bulk of the dec_tps gain lives. Tier 1 will refine
the priority order; the items below are the candidates.

#### 3.2.1 MoE Q4_K GEMV — port llama.cpp's hot-path techniques (1–2 weeks)

llama.cpp's `mul_mat_q4_K_f32` Metal kernel is the gold standard for
Q4_K bandwidth on Apple Silicon. We have a port from Phase 1B
(`gemv_q4_k_m_llama_port`) that landed opt-in only at +0% e2e on
attention shapes. **Re-investigate at MoE shapes** which we now
believe are the actual hot path.

Specific techniques to validate / port:

- **Vectorized loads** (`packed_uchar4` → `simd_load`) — llama.cpp
  reads 4 bytes per simd lane, we read 1. Should give ~2× memory
  throughput on the Q4 dequant path.
- **Block-tiled threadgroup memory** — load the activation vector
  once into threadgroup, reuse across all dequantized blocks. We
  currently re-read the activation per block.
- **simdgroup_matrix_multiply 8×8** for the dequant→matmul fused
  path. Hardware simdgroup matmul ops on M3 are 8× faster than
  scalar fmuladd.
- **Per-shape kernel selection** — autotune already supports this;
  we just need MoE-shape entries in the autotune sweep (currently
  attention-shape only).

Bench gate per change: `bench-kernel --kernel moe_q4_k_gemv --shape
1408x2048 --iterations 200` must show ≥1.3× speedup; then end-to-end
must clear +5% e2e. **Both** gates required to flip default.

If the Phase 1B port already had the right techniques but was tested
on the wrong shapes, this could land in days.

#### 3.2.2 MoE expert weight prefetching (1 week)

Currently each layer's MoE picks 6 of 64 experts at runtime. The
weight reads are demand-loaded; M3 Pro's prefetcher doesn't know
which experts are coming.

Approach: for layer N, after gate router fires, kick off async
`madvise(WILLNEED)` on layer N+1's likely experts. Prediction can be:
(a) the same experts as layer N, or (b) a tiny per-layer expert-frequency
table built at engine load.

This doesn't increase bandwidth utilization but can hide the latency
of cold expert page-faults (relevant especially when running near
RAM budget on 18 GB).

Expected: small win on V2-Lite (mostly RAM-resident already), larger
on Mixtral.

Bench gate: +3% e2e on V2-Lite, +20% on Mixtral.

#### 3.2.3 LM head — actually wire the things we already built (3 days)

Phase 5B.2 built a top-K LM head kernel that was never wired into the
generate path (we removed it during cleanup; need to bring it back
*and wire it*).

Plan:
- Restore the kernel from git history (commit `6b4c95a`).
- Wire it into `forward_token_final_norm`'s LM head dispatch.
- Profile flag `lm_head_top_k=8192` opt-in; greedy sampling already
  only needs the top-1, so K=8192 is sampling-safe with temperature.
- Bench gate: +2% e2e on V2-Lite at K=8192. If it lands, flip default.

Cheap, mechanical, near-zero risk. Should have shipped in v1.1.0.

#### 3.2.4 Attention projection kernel fusion (1 week)

q_a/kv_a/q_b/o_proj are four separate Q4_K GEMV dispatches per layer.
They share the input activation (q_a/kv_a both read x; q_b/o_proj
both read post-MLA tensors). Fuse into:

- `qkv_a_fused` — single kernel doing q_a + kv_a from x, writing two
  outputs. Halves activation reads.
- `qkv_b_fused` — q_b + o_proj from a shared input.

Approach: the fused kernel is structurally identical to two GEMV
calls reading from the same input — extend `gemv_q4_k_m_v2` to take
two weight tensors and write two outputs. Threadgroup memory for the
shared input is reused across both outputs.

Bench gate: ≥1.5× per-call speedup on `bench-kernel --kernel
qkv_a_fused`; +3% e2e to flip default.

#### 3.2.5 Eliminate redundant rmsnorm reads (3 days)

Each layer does:
```
rmsnorm → q_a → ...
rmsnorm → kv_a → ...   (same input as q_a's rmsnorm!)
```

The rmsnorm of x is computed twice per layer because q_a and kv_a have
different paths. Cache the rmsnorm output in a per-layer scratch buffer
and reuse.

Trivial change once identified, ~50 LoC.

Bench gate: +1% e2e (small per-layer save × 27 layers = ~2-3%).

#### 3.2.6 fp16 throughout the residual (1 week, contingent)

Phase 5C2 added fp16 x_norm; we kept it opt-in because it didn't
compound. With Tier 1's GPU counter data we'll see whether fp16
residual reduces the activation read footprint enough to compound
with the MoE GEMV port.

Hypothesis: post-port (with bandwidth utilization at 50%+), the
activation reads become a meaningful share of bandwidth. fp16
residual halves them. Compound effect could be +5%.

Bench gate: +5% combined with port. If yes, flip both defaults.

| item | est. e2e gain | risk |
|---|---:|---|

**Estimate**: 3–5 weeks. Heavy parallelization possible — most items

**Tier 2 close target**: 17 → **25–34 dec_tps**.

### Tier 3 — architectural levers (4–6 weeks, target +30–50% on Tier 2 base)

These are the items the v2.1 sketch focused on. With Tier 2 closing
the bandwidth gap, Tier 3 attacks the remaining structural overhead.

#### 3.3.1 Apple Neural Engine — LM head + attention projections (3-4 weeks)

Per `docs/v2.1_architecture_sketch.md`. With Tier 1's GPU timing in
hand, we will know whether ANE can actually beat the (post-Tier-2)
Metal LM head and attention projections. The sketch's 5 sub-phases
remain valid:

- 2.1A FFI shim (1-2 weeks)
- 2.1B Build LM head Core ML model in memory (1 week)
- 2.1C Wire ANE LM head into forward (1 week)
- 2.1D Extend to attention projections (1-2 weeks)
- 2.1E Close + bench (0.5 day)

**Bench gate change vs the sketch**: Tier 1's GPU counters give us a
real per-call comparison (ANE vs Metal). The sketch's 1.5× gate
remains, but now we can attribute precisely whether a miss is
ANE-call-overhead or matmul-itself.

**Realistic gain post-Tier-2**: +10-20% e2e if ANE LM head + attn
projections both land. Smaller than the sketch's projection because
Tier 2 will have already optimized those kernels.

#### 3.3.2 Metal IndirectCommandBuffer — pre-built CBs (2 weeks)

Currently we encode the full forward pass into a fresh
TokenCommandBuffer every token. With ICB, the CB is built once at
engine load (per fixed-shape configuration) and re-submitted with
just the input/output buffer pointers swapped per token.

Gain: eliminates the ~2.4 ms/token CB encoding overhead. Per the
architecture audit, that's the 4.1% dispatch slice — ICB closes
~3-4% of it (some unavoidable sync remains).

Foundation work: the KernelArgBuffer refactor from v1.1.0 was
specifically to enable ICB. We have the prerequisite.

Risk: ICB requires *all* kernel inputs to be argument-buffer addressable
(no per-call `set_bytes`). Refactor effort is ~1 week of mechanical
porting plus ICB plumbing.

Bench gate: +3% e2e (matches the audit's projection).

#### 3.3.3 AMX coprocessor for sampling + small matmuls (1 week)

The Apple Matrix Coprocessor is accessed via Accelerate framework
(`vDSP_*`). Used by BLAS internally. Wins available on:

- **Sampling** (`vDSP_maxvi` for argmax, `vDSP_vsmul` for top-K):
  currently runs on GPU, has a CPU-readback round-trip. Moving to
  CPU/AMX eliminates the round-trip.
- **Gate router matmul** (small Q4_K, ~1 ms): AMX likely beats GPU
  for this size due to dispatch overhead.

Add `accelerate-sys` (Apple-shipped, no external dep). Implement
behind profile flag. Bench gate: +2% e2e (small per-call ops × many
calls per token).

#### 3.3.4 Multi-layer fused TCB (2 weeks, contingent on ICB)

Phase 4 close recommended fusing 2-4 layers into one CB to amortize
the commit overhead. With ICB this becomes natural — the entire
forward pass is one ICB submission already.

If we go ICB-only this is subsumed. If ICB blocks, this is a
fallback that captures a fraction of the same gain.

Bench gate: +2% e2e (only relevant if not doing ICB).

| item | est. e2e gain (on Tier 2 base) | risk |
|---|---:|---|
| ANE LM head | +5–10% | high (40% lands cleanly) |
| ANE attention projections | +5–10% | high |
| Metal IndirectCommandBuffer | +3–5% | medium |
| AMX sampling/argmax/gate | +2–3% | low |
| **Tier 3 stack (compounding)** | **+15–30%** | medium-high |

**Estimate**: 4–6 weeks. ANE work is the bulk (sketch already covers
it). ICB is parallel-dispatchable to a Sonnet worker once the
KernelArgBuffer audit is done.

**Tier 3 close target**: 25-34 → **30–45 dec_tps**.

### Tier 4 — speculative decode rebuild (4–6 weeks, contingent)

Tier 4 is the architectural rewrite that v1.1.0 Phase 4 promised but
didn't deliver: true K-parallel compute for batched verify. This is
**not on the critical path to 30 dec_tps** — Tiers 1+2+3 already
cover that. Tier 4 is for pushing past 40.

#### 3.4.1 True K-parallel batched forward (3-4 weeks)

Rewrite `forward_tokens_batched_tcb` so K queries actually process in
parallel across SMs, not sequentially in CB.

What needs to change:
- **Batched MLA decode kernel**: `mla_decode_metal_batched_slots`
  exists from Phase 4A but processes K slots sequentially in inner
  loop. Rewrite as K-parallel grid (each threadgroup handles one
  query slot).
- **Batched MoE GEMV**: NEW kernel. Currently MoE expert dispatch is
  per-token. Need a K-token MoE GEMV that processes K tokens'
  active-expert lists in one kernel launch (different tokens may
  route to different experts — non-trivial).
- **Batched rmsnorm + residual + everything else**: extend to K-token
  in shape.
- **DecodeArena buffer separation**: K separate x_buf/q_buf
  allocations (Phase 5A added this scaffold but the per-layer
  kernels still process serially). Actually use the K-axis.

Risk: high. The MoE batched GEMV is genuinely new kernel work; the
expert routing per token means workgroups have heterogeneous
weight-read patterns, which may not parallelize as cleanly as a
dense K-batch matmul.

Bench gate: at α=1.0 on repetitive (the prior smoking gun), spec
decode must show ≥1.5× e2e. (Currently it's −0.5%.)

#### 3.4.2 Distilled draft model (3-4 weeks, parallel workstream)

Train a 1B-2B distillation of V2-Lite. Use it as the spec draft
model. Expected α=0.4-0.6 on natural English vs current α=0.05 (n-gram).

This requires PyTorch + dataset + GPU compute outside of dismantle's
in-session work. Manager (you) handles training; dismantle provides
runtime hooks (`SpeculateMode::Distilled` accepting a second model
path).

If both 3.4.1 and 3.4.2 land: +30-50% e2e on natural English (α=0.5
× K=4 batched verify gain).

#### Tier 4 deliverables

Conditional on commitment to a 4-6 week architectural project. If
Tiers 1+2+3 hit 35+ dec_tps, Tier 4 is optional. If they stall at
25-30, Tier 4 is the remaining path to 40+.

Even the pessimistic case gets us to 22 — measurably better than

inside striking distance of llama.cpp.

60 is plausible only if Tier 4 lands cleanly. That's a 4-month
project. We should not promise it; we should commit to Tiers 1+2+3
and reassess after Tier 2's measurements come in.

## 6 — Bench-first commit gates (the same rule, applied throughout)

Every change with a perf claim follows the bench-first gate:

```
TRIALS=4 TOKENS=24 bash tools/bench/coexist_bench.sh    # before
# ... apply change on a worktree ...
TRIALS=4 TOKENS=24 bash tools/bench/coexist_bench.sh    # after
bash tools/bench/bench_diff.sh HEAD~1 HEAD              # significance
```

Gate thresholds:

| change type | per-kernel gate | e2e gate |
|---|---|---|
| Kernel correctness fix (no perf claim) | parity test | none |
| Kernel optimization | bench-kernel ≥1.3× | e2e ≥+5% to flip default |
| Foundation refactor (e.g. ICB plumbing) | parity preserved | e2e ≥−2% (no regression) |
| Architectural rebuild (Tier 4) | parity preserved | e2e ≥+15% to ship |

If a gate fails: the change lands as opt-in only, with a halt-doc
explaining the measurement and what attended work could close the gap.
Never flip a default that doesn't clear +5% e2e on the default
workload (V2-Lite Q4_K_M coexist median).

This is the rule that protected v2.0 from shipping +0.86% as a real
gain. Same rule applies in v2.1.

## 7 — Failure modes and fallbacks

### 7.1 Tier 1 takes longer than 1 week

If GPU counter plumbing snags on Apple's API quirks (it might —
MTLCounterSampleBuffer's documentation is thin), allow +3 days. If
still blocked, fall back to Apple's Instruments Metal System Trace
template: capture .gputrace files manually for representative runs,
extract per-kernel time by hand. Slower iteration but unblocks Tier 2.

### 7.2 MoE Q4_K port doesn't deliver in Tier 2

Tier 1's llama.cpp comparison may show our kernel is *already* close
to llama.cpp's per kernel — and the gap is elsewhere (e.g. memory
allocator, page-fault rate, command queue scheduling). In that case:

- Skip the port re-investigation
- Re-prioritize Tier 2 around fusion + redundant-read elimination
- Likely smaller Tier 2 gain (+20-30% instead of +50-100%)
- Tier 3 ANE becomes more important

### 7.3 ANE proves infeasible in Tier 3

Per the v2.1 sketch's own contingency: pivot to AMX-only (smaller
gain, lower risk) or ship Tier 1+2 as v2.1 and frame ANE as v2.2.

### 7.4 We hit the bandwidth ceiling at 35 dec_tps

If after Tier 1+2+3 we measure GPU bandwidth utilization at 70%+ and
e2e at 35, we've hit the practical ceiling on this hardware for this
model. Pushing further requires:

- Smaller model (V2-Lite is already small for ANE/AMX considerations)
- Lower quant (Q3_K, INT4 activations) — quality cost
- Different model (smaller MoE or dense)

That's a v3.0 conversation, not a v2.1 one. Document the ceiling and
ship.

### 7.5 Tier 4 batched verify rebuild stalls

If true K-parallel forward proves architecturally hard (heterogeneous
expert routing per K token is the hardest part), fall back to:

- Distilled draft model only (gets some α gain even with sequential
  verify, but smaller)
- Document the architectural limitation and park spec decode formally

Tier 4 is the highest-risk tier. Don't commit to it before Tier 1+2+3
deliver.

## 9 — What this plan does NOT promise

- 60 dec_tps. That's an optimistic outcome contingent on every tier
  landing well plus Tier 4. The realistic projection is 30-40.
- Beating llama.cpp. We're closing the gap, not necessarily passing
  it. Pure-Rust + small codebase remains the differentiator.
- Linear progress. Tiers compound multiplicatively. A bad Tier 2 (low
  bandwidth gain) caps the total stack regardless of Tier 3's success.
- Calendar precision. 8-12 weeks is the realistic envelope; surprises
  may push to 14. The bench-first commit gate ensures we don't
  ship regressions, which means slow tiers stall gracefully.

What this plan **does** promise:

- Honest measurement before optimization (Tier 1)
- Compound optimization with bench-first gates throughout
- Realistic ceiling math (we know what 70% bandwidth utilization looks like)
- Failure modes with documented fallbacks
